# Case Study Structure

Each case study follows this format:

1. Tagline

2. Overview
   name of client, what they do and their location

3. Challenge/Problem: (**or for personal projects: **The Project\*\*)
   What did the client ask you to do?  What was the briefing? What was the main challenge and measure of success? Did you have a certain idea or expectation for the project when you began?

4. My Role:
   I worked as… responsible for …
   I created …
   I also worked with… Another team conducted…

5. Impact/ Outcome:
   Why the project was successful or meaningful. Or, if it was not completed, why I still love this work and what I learned from it.

6. Steps
   [Narrative the design experience through sequential key approaches/steps/activities/outputs. Each has a headline, short writeup and captioned imagery]

7. Final Thoughts (optional)
   Lessons learned or next steps

8. TL;DR
   This short summary is written for time-strapped hiring managers and recruiters who want a quick snapshot of what I did and why it matters. The goal is to clearly convey scope, impact, and my role in under 75 words—before they decide whether to scroll deeper into the case study.

Below is an example to emulate, in terms of organization, tone, and length:

"Tagline:
**Modernizing fleet management with targeted UI improvements and a scalable, standardized design system**

**Overview:**

**RyderGyde** is a digital platform developed by **Ryder,** a leading logistics and transportation company, that helps fleet managers monitor, maintain, and manage commercial vehicles through connected services and real-time insights.

Over a six-week engagement, I was contracted to lead their product experience refresh, starting with a comprehensive UI/UX audit to identify design inconsistencies and key user pain points. Leveraging MUI as a foundational component library, I customized branded styles for data visualization widgets, data tables, and geospatial maps for real-time fleet tracking. This refreshed design system enabled the internal team to continue resolving UX issues without friction—avoiding inconsistencies in both component behavior and visual style.

**Challenge:**

RyderGyde’s 8-year-old platform needed a visual and UX refresh to stay competitive in the logistics tech space. The design was outdated, lacked clarity, and didn’t align with modern SaaS best practices. Freight managers struggled to access critical details—like maintenance status, recent documents, or long-term vehicle value—due to a cluttered and disjointed UI with many dead ends in the experience.

We were brought in to modernize the UI, create a reusable data visualization system for upcoming “Insights” features, and improve dashboard usability—while working within tight dev timelines.

**My Role:**

I worked as the lead Product Designer, responsible for refreshing the RyderGyde platform’s UI and establishing a foundational design system to support future development. I led a small team of two product designers, including myself, throughout the engagement.

Working closely with another UI designer and guided by product priorities we identified with Ryder stakeholders, we modernized the visual language of the freight management platform.

Throughout the project, we partnered closely with engineering, product, and marketing teams to integrate design smoothly into the product roadmap. This collaboration helped align priorities, streamline implementation, and ensure technical feasibility at every step.

**Impact/ Outcome:**

In just 6 weeks, we delivered a branded design system that gave the Ryder team a standardized foundation to solve key UX issues we outlined in the audit. Built on MUI, the UI kit streamlined design-dev collaboration, accelerated implementation, and laid the groundwork for designers to deliver a more intuitive fleet management experience for their customers.

**Steps**

### **Building a Scalable Design System**

To bring visual consistency and scalability to RyderGyde, we built a cohesive design system using Figma best practices. By leveraging variables for modes, breakpoints, and semantic values for type, color, and effects—and aligning with MUI—we standardized components across the platform with branded styles for color, typography, and spacing to ensure a unified experience.

---

### **A Targeted Approach to UI Redesign**

Instead of overhauling entire flows, we focused on targeted UI improvements. We customized the MUI-based component library to reflect Ryder’s brand and applied it to key elements like tables, cards, and dashboards—enhancing usability, improving content density, and aligning interactions with familiar patterns.

---

### **Filter UI Explorations**

We explored several UI options for filtering vehicle data, including a combo box dropdown, a filter drawer, and grouped select menus using MUI X. Due to technical constraints and timeline limitations, the team opted to ship an out-of-the-box solution first, with plans to implement a more customized version in future iterations.

---

### **Designing a Geospatial Experience**

We introduced a map-based UI to help freight managers locate vehicles based on their shop status (e.g., “Ready for Pickup”) or active roadside events. This interactive view enables faster decision-making and direct access to Ryder services—connecting vehicle location with real-time operational needs.
